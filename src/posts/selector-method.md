# I Used to Make the News Make Sense. Now I Do That for Products.

For years my job was to take chaotic, fast moving, high stakes information and make it land clearly for a mass audience in real time. There was no waiting for the perfect framework. You had minutes, sometimes seconds, to find the actual story buried inside the noise and get it out in a way people could understand and act on.

That job does not exist on a resume template. But it turns out it is exactly the skill product marketing has been asking for the whole time.

## The Skill Nobody Names

Most product marketing training teaches you frameworks. Personas, messaging hierarchies, positioning statements. All useful. None of it teaches you the thing that actually matters most, which is how to find the one true thing buried inside everything a company is trying to say about itself.

That is not a framework skill. That is a newsroom skill. It is the ability to sit inside chaos, resist the urge to organize it too early, and wait for the actual signal to surface.

In music, the person who does this is called a Selector. Not the performer. Not even always the most technically skilled musician in the room. The Selector is the one who knows which piece goes where, what comes next, and how to keep a room moving without ever breaking the flow. They are not creating the music. They are finding the connections inside music that already exists and putting them in an order that makes people feel something they could not get to on their own.

That is what I do for products and brands. I am not creating your product's value. It is already there. I am finding the connection inside everything you have already built and putting it in the order that makes the right person stop and say, that is for me.

## How It Actually Works

The work always starts the same way, and it never starts with me talking.

I let the client pitch me first. Not the polished investor version. The real one. What you think is working, what you think is broken, who you think your audience is. That conversation is not small talk before the real work begins. It is the first and most important piece of research I will do, because what someone believes about their own product reveals more than any competitive audit ever could.

From there it is research. Audience, competitors, the language already being used, the gaps nobody named because they were standing too close to see them. This is the crate digging part. Unglamorous, unscripted, and the part most people skip because it does not feel like progress.

Then comes the part that looks like magic from the outside but is really just pattern recognition done with full attention. The moment where the research, the client's own words, and the gap in the market all line up into one clear story. That is the aha moment. Not because something new was invented, but because something that was already true finally got said out loud in the right order.

## Why This Matters More Than a Framework

Frameworks are useful after you know what you are trying to say. They are dangerous before that, because they let you skip the hard part and fill in the blanks with assumptions instead of truth.

A Selector does not start with the framework. They start with the room. What is the energy here. What does this crowd actually need right now, not what the set list says they should want. The framework comes after the read, not before it.

That is the difference between marketing that sounds right and marketing that actually moves people. One was built from a template. The other was built from paying real attention.

## The Work Continues

This is not a one time trick. The goal was never a single good moment, a clever tagline, one good campaign. The goal is the same as a great DJ set. You do not stop after the first song that gets people moving. You build the whole night, track by track, until the room does not even notice the hours have passed.

That is what good product marketing should feel like for a brand. Not a single insight. A flow that keeps building.
