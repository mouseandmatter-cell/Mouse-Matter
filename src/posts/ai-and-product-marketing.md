# AI Has a Place. So Do the Humans Companies Keep Cutting to Make Room for It.

I built the Mouse & Matter website using AI and honestly it felt weird at first.

Not because I was ashamed of it. Because I am used to needing a whole team to build something like this. A designer. A developer. A strategist. And here I was giving input on everything. The colors. The visuals. The flow. Watching it come together in real time and making changes immediately. It was one of the stranger experiences I have had in a while and I say that as someone who has done a lot of strange things professionally.

But what stayed with me after was not the speed or the convenience. It was understanding what I actually did in that process and what AI actually did. Because those are not the same thing and most companies are getting that confused right now.

## What AI Actually Did

AI did not come up with Mouse & Matter. It did not decide the brand should feel like a Selector. It did not know that the playlist needed to be called Find Your Flow or that the pull quote needed to stop you not just fill space. It did not sit with me through round after round of language until something finally felt true instead of just correct.

It built what I told it to build. Fast. Accurate. Without complaint.

If I had the budget for a real designer I would have hired one. Not because AI did a bad job but because a real designer brings things you never thought to ask for. The easter egg nobody requested. The detail that makes someone screenshot something and send it to a friend. You cannot prompt for that. That is taste. That is instinct. That is years of paying attention to things most people walk past. AI has pattern matching. That is not the same thing.

## The Productivity Story Does Not Add Up

Companies are cutting teams and calling it an AI strategy. The honest version of that story is a lot messier than they are letting on.

A lot of these companies are not actually more productive. They are spending more as AI tools move to token based pricing that scales with how much you rely on them. The cost did not disappear. It moved. And in a lot of cases it grew. Meanwhile the people who used to do that work are gone. The ones with context. With judgment. With relationships. With years of pattern recognition that was never written down anywhere because it lived in their heads. What is left is a faster tool and a thinner team trying to catch everything the tool cannot see.

That is not efficiency. That is a bet. And a lot of companies are quietly losing it while still telling the story like it worked.

## What AI Is Actually Good For

AI is good at giving people access. That part is real and worth protecting.

A founder who could never afford a strategist now has something that can help them think out loud. Organize a mess of ideas. Draft something they can react to instead of starting from nothing. That matters especially for people who have been locked out of expensive expertise their whole careers.

The problem is not that AI exists. The problem is when companies use it to replace judgment instead of extend it. There is a real difference between a tool that helps a sharp person move faster and a tool that gets handed the parts of the job nobody wants to pay a human to keep doing.

## Where Product Marketing Lives That AI Cannot Reach

I was at a conference recently. The session was about AI. And I watched the faces in the room.

There were people whose expressions clearly said they had no idea what AI actually was or was not. And then someone made a joke about keeping their current systems running on spit and hope. The laugh that came from that was not a laugh about a bad product. It was the laugh of people who recognized themselves. Who felt seen. Who were telling me without saying a word exactly what they needed to hear.

No AI in that room caught that. I did.

That is product marketing. Reading what is not being said. Catching the joke that reveals the real pain. Knowing which person in the room needs to be challenged and which one needs to feel heard. Building the kind of trust that makes someone tell you the actual problem instead of the polished version.

AI can help me move faster once I know what I am building. It cannot tell me what is true about an audience that has not said it out loud yet. That part is still entirely human and I do not think that changes anytime soon no matter how good the tools get.

So yes I used AI to build this site. The thinking, the voice, the judgment about what mattered and what did not, that was mine. That is the difference companies keep blurring when they talk about what AI replaced.

It did not replace the thinking. It just got faster at typing it out.

Protect the thinking. That is the part worth paying for.
